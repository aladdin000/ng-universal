See the big picture and develop leadership skills

Earning the Energy Risk Professional (ERP) designation can help differentiate you in an increasingly competitive market. If you have little experience or a narrowly focused background in energy, the ERP Program can help fill gaps in your knowledge. Exposure to the broad set of topics covered in the ERP Exam curriculum will build the foundation of knowledge and market insights necessary to see the “big picture” and ask the right questions as you develop your career in energy.

As a Certified ERP you will be prepared for jobs with increasing breadth and responsibility, including senior management positions that require the ability to assess and manage risk strategically to create value for your organization.

As a hiring manager, you can use the ERP certification as a benchmark for identifying potential new hires who possess the skills necessary to become future leaders of your organization.

Apply your knowledge to a diverse set of career paths in energy

The knowledge and skills developed while earning the ERP designation can be applied across a diverse set of functional roles within firms that operate directly and indirectly in the energy market.

Industry Opportunities

  • Risk management within integrated petroleum companies, electricity generators and power utilities, and renewable energy companies: Evaluate risks associated with the upstream production, transport, and processing of oil, gas, and coal; the generation, distribution, and marketing of electricity; and the financing and integration of renewable generation facilities.
  • Commodity trading, scheduling, structuring and origination: Apply knowledge of physical transport, storage, and product processing to assess market opportunities.
  • Consulting: Leverage insights about the energy market value chain to satisfy client objectives across a variety of projects, including technology applications, strategic planning, and financial analysis.
  • Research and investment management: Apply knowledge of market fundamentals and technical trends to inform investment decisions.
  • Financial control: Apply analytical skills and market perspective to understand how the full scope of commercial operations are aggregated in complex accounting, finance, and reporting systems.
  • Treasury: Assess company liquidity policies and manage corporate funding needs.
  • Audit: Examine adequacy of firm’s risk controls and processes based on best practices, regulatory requirements, and jurisdictional structures.
  • Regulatory compliance: Understand the market rationale for proposed regulations and their impact on business models.
